Defensive Publication: Rotary Piston Engine With Oscillating Combustion Chamber Backstop

Abstract: This disclosure describes a rotary piston internal combustion engine that uses an oscillating combustion chamber backstop to achieve high compression, controlled sealing, and separation of compression and expansion phases. The design incorporates a one‑way intake valve to ensure proper airflow direction and prevent combustion products from escaping through the intake. A piston‑shaped combustion chamber and piston‑shaped rotor geometry are used to improve sealing, addressing a long‑standing limitation of rotary engines. This document is intended to place the described concepts into the public domain as prior art.

Technical Field

This invention relates to internal combustion engines, specifically rotary piston engines with dynamically sealed combustion chambers and separated compression/expansion cycles.

Background

Traditional rotary engines (such as the Wankel) suffer from chronic sealing issues due to their elongated, trochoidal combustion chambers and apex seal geometry. These shapes inherently make it difficult to maintain uniform sealing pressure, resulting in blow‑by, poor compression, and incomplete combustion.
This disclosure presents an alternative architecture that improves sealing, compression, and combustion uniformity through an oscillating backstop mechanism and a piston‑shaped combustion chamber.

Note: The biggest engineering challenge for rotary piston engines is achieving a reliable seal between the moving parts and the chamber. Without an effective seal, gases can leak, leading to poor efficiency and performance. This is the primary reason why such designs are not widely used in practice, despite their theoretical advantages.

Summary of the Design

The engine consists of a rotary piston housed within a circular chamber. A movable backstop oscillates into position to temporarily seal the combustion chamber during compression and combustion. This allows for significantly higher compression ratios than typical rotary engines.
The combustion chamber and the rotary piston are shaped more like a conventional reciprocating piston — compact, cylindrical, and symmetric — rather than elongated or wedge‑shaped. This geometry enables tighter sealing and more consistent pressure distribution.
Expansion occurs in a separate chamber downstream of the combustion event.

Key Features

  1. Oscillating Combustion Chamber Backstop
    • A mechanical backstop moves into position to form a temporary, high‑integrity seal during compression and combustion.
    • The backstop is oriented perpendicularly to the rotary motion.
    • This enables higher compression ratios and more controlled combustion.
  2. Piston‑Shaped Rotary Element for Improved Sealing
    • The rotary piston is shaped like a traditional reciprocating piston rather than a thin, elongated rotor.
    • This geometry allows for a tighter, more uniform seal — directly addressing the standard sealing problem found in rotary engines.
    • The piston‑like shape reduces surface area variation and improves chamber symmetry, enhancing compression stability.
  3. Split‑Cycle Operation
    • Compression and combustion occur in one chamber.
    • Expansion occurs in a separate chamber, improving efficiency and reducing thermal stress.
  4. One‑Way Air Intake Valve
    • The air intake includes a one‑way valve to ensure it functions strictly as an intake.
    • This prevents unburned fuel or combustion gases from escaping through the intake port.
    • Ensures proper airflow direction and cycle integrity.
  5. Sealing Strategy
    • Sealing may be achieved through lubrication, precision tolerances, or both.
    • A tight seal ensures that unburned fuel is carried into the next cycle rather than lost.

Operation Overview

  1. Intake Phase: Fresh air enters through the one‑way intake valve.
  2. Compression Phase: The rotary piston compresses the air as the backstop moves into sealing position.
  3. Combustion Phase: Autoignition or spark ignition occurs within the sealed chamber.
  4. Expansion Phase: The backstop opens and combustion gases are directed to a turbine or piston‑based expansion stage.
  5. Cycle Continuity: Any unburned fuel remains within the system and participates in the next cycle.

Advantages Over Existing Designs

  • Higher achievable compression ratios.
  • Improved sealing due to piston‑shaped geometry.
  • More uniform combustion due to chamber shape.
  • Reduced fuel loss.
  • Separation of compression and expansion allows optimized thermodynamic conditions for each phase.
Conclusion: This disclosure places the described rotary piston engine architecture — including the oscillating combustion chamber backstop, piston‑shaped rotary element, one‑way intake valve, and split‑cycle operation — into the public domain as prior art. Any future patent applications attempting to claim these concepts should be considered non‑novel in light of this publication.
